# Peptips — Brand Book

The voice, audience, and editorial standards for peptips.com — the most genuinely helpful GLP-1 information resource on the internet.

---

## 1. Who we are

Peptips is the friend who already went through it. We answer the questions that the prescribing doctor didn't have time to answer, the questions that feel too embarrassing to ask, and the questions that don't have great answers anywhere else on the internet right now.

We are not selling a drug. We are not affiliated with any clinic. We are not trying to scare people away from GLP-1s and we are not trying to sell them as a miracle. We're trying to help the millions of people who are *already on these medications* navigate a year of their life that nobody prepared them for.

The market is enormous and growing. The information is fragmented, often wrong, and split between scary medical journals and Reddit threads. Peptips is the third option: warm, accurate, calm, cited.

## 2. The reader

### Primary persona — "Jen, 42"

She's a project manager at a healthcare company. She has two kids, ages 8 and 11. She got her semaglutide prescription three weeks ago. Her doctor spent maybe 12 minutes with her at the appointment, handed her a pamphlet, told her to start at 0.25mg, and said "see you in three months."

She's lost 3 pounds in three weeks, which she's happy about. But she also:
- Threw up the second night and is terrified it'll happen again
- Is constipated for the first time in her adult life
- Feels weirdly disconnected from food in a way that's both relieving and unsettling
- Doesn't know if she should be lifting weights to preserve muscle (heard about this on a podcast)
- Doesn't know if she can drink wine on her sister's birthday next weekend
- Has been Googling "Ozempic burps" at 11pm with growing alarm
- Read three Reddit threads that contradicted each other on whether to take her shot in the morning or at night

She wants:
- A real answer, written by a real person
- Without medical jargon she has to translate
- Without being lectured
- Without being told to "ask your doctor" (her doctor is unreachable for 8 weeks)
- Without being upsold a $400/month coaching program
- Without being shamed for being on the drug at all

She trusts:
- Her own gut (eventually)
- Other women who've been on the drug
- A few specific Instagram accounts (mostly registered dietitians who post real food advice)
- The drug manufacturer's website (cautiously)
- Sources that cite their sources

She does not trust:
- Telehealth clinics that pop up everywhere
- "Wellness" influencers with no credentials
- Reddit (anymore)
- Random listicle sites
- Anything that uses the phrase "miracle drug"

### Secondary personas

- **"Mike, 51"** — male, type 2 diabetic on Mounjaro for 14 months, plateaued, looking for what comes next
- **"Lauren, 36"** — pre-diabetic, on compounded semaglutide from a telehealth clinic, worried about supply continuity
- **"Diane, 58"** — postmenopausal, just started Wegovy, dealing with hair thinning and looking for management strategies
- **"Cara, 29"** — PCOS, thinking about asking her doctor for a prescription, researching first

We write for Jen by default. The other personas are addressed in specific posts targeting their needs.

## 3. The promise

> Real answers about life on a GLP-1, without the hype or the lectures.

Three things every Peptips post must deliver:

1. **A direct answer to the question, in the first 100 words** — no preamble, no "in this post we'll explore"
2. **The why, with real sources** — every claim cited to a manufacturer label, a peer-reviewed paper, or a regulatory document
3. **The "what to actually do"** — practical, specific, non-prescriptive guidance that respects the reader's autonomy and her relationship with her doctor

## 4. Voice

### How we sound
- **Warm.** Like a friend, not a clinic. We use "you," not "patients."
- **Patient.** We assume the reader doesn't know things, but we don't talk down.
- **Specific.** "0.25mg in week 1, escalating to 0.5mg in week 5" — not "your doctor will gradually increase your dose."
- **Calm about scary things.** When discussing side effects, we describe them factually and offer practical management — never alarm.
- **Honest about uncertainty.** When the literature is mixed or thin, we say so.
- **Respectful of the reader's autonomy.** We don't tell people what to do. We tell them what we know and what the trade-offs are.

### How we don't sound
- ❌ Clinical-detached ("Patients on semaglutide may experience gastrointestinal adverse events")
- ❌ Hype-y ("The MIRACLE drug everyone's talking about!")
- ❌ Influencer-coded ("OMG bestie, this drug changed my LIFE")
- ❌ Lecturing ("You should NEVER drink alcohol on...")
- ❌ Fear-mongering ("Hidden DANGERS of Ozempic doctors won't tell you")
- ❌ Salesy ("Click here to get your prescription today!")
- ❌ Mommy-blog ("Hey friends!")

### Side-by-side examples

**❌ Don't write this:**
> Are you experiencing the dreaded Ozempic burps?? You're not alone girl! Sulfur burps are SUPER common on semaglutide and they can be SO embarrassing. Here are 10 mom-approved tricks to make them stop! 💗

**✅ Write this:**
> Sulfur burps — the rotten-egg smell — are common on semaglutide, especially during the first few weeks and after dose increases. They happen because the drug slows gastric emptying, which gives food more time to ferment in the stomach. They're not dangerous, but they're miserable. Here's what tends to help, what doesn't, and when to call your doctor.

---

**❌ Don't write this:**
> Studies have shown that GLP-1 receptor agonists may be associated with increased risk of muscle mass loss. Patients are advised to consult their physician regarding appropriate exercise interventions.

**✅ Write this:**
> When you lose weight on a GLP-1, somewhere between 25% and 40% of what you lose is lean mass — not just fat. (The STEP-1 trial found about 39% lean mass loss; the SURMOUNT-1 trial reported around 25%.) That's higher than most people lose with diet alone. The two interventions with the strongest evidence for protecting lean mass while losing weight on a GLP-1: hitting a protein target around 1.2-1.6g per kilogram of body weight per day, and resistance training 2-3 times per week. Neither is a guarantee. Both move the needle.

---

**❌ Don't write this:**
> NEVER drink alcohol while taking Ozempic. It can cause serious side effects.

**✅ Write this:**
> The official Ozempic prescribing information doesn't prohibit alcohol, but two things are worth knowing. First: alcohol on a slowed-empty stomach hits harder and lasts longer than you're used to — many people on semaglutide report feeling drunk on a single glass of wine. Second: alcohol can worsen the GI side effects, especially nausea. If you want to drink, drink less, drink slower, eat first, and don't drive home expecting your usual tolerance.

## 6. Editorial principles (Peptips-specific, on top of network principles)

1. **Frame as informational, never as medical advice.** Every post that discusses a specific drug, dose, or protocol carries a top-of-page note: *"This post is for informational purposes only and is not medical advice. Always discuss any changes to your treatment with your healthcare provider."*

2. **Cite manufacturer labels and trial data.** For any claim about a drug's mechanism, dosing, side effect rate, or efficacy — cite the FDA prescribing information or the published trial. No exceptions.

3. **Use generic names alongside brand names.** "Semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy)" and "tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound)" — always both, especially in the first mention of a post. This is both for SEO and for accuracy.

4. **Never recommend a specific telehealth provider.** We can describe the telehealth landscape neutrally, but we do not name specific providers as recommendations. (Future: when injectcompass launches, that's where comparison content lives — and even there, framed as reference, not recommendation.)

5. **Acknowledge cost.** GLP-1s are expensive. Insurance coverage is messy. We address it directly when relevant, without judgment.

6. **No before/after content. No weight loss stories.** Both are platform compliance landmines (Instagram, Pinterest, Facebook all penalize weight loss imagery) and they invite the wrong reader. We talk about side effects, food, lifestyle, plateaus — not pounds.

7. **Steel-man the alternative view.** When discussing whether to start a GLP-1, we present the case against (cost, side effects, long-term unknowns, weight regain after stopping) honestly.

8. **Update aggressively when new data drops.** This is a fast-moving field. New trials publish constantly. When something changes, the relevant pillar gets updated within a week.

9. **No fear-mongering, no hype.** Both extremes drive engagement. Neither serves the reader.

## 12. The line we never cross

Peptips exists to help people. The moment we start pushing readers toward a specific drug, a specific clinic, or a specific outcome, we lose the trust that makes the site valuable.

Specifically:
- We do not tell readers to start a GLP-1
- We do not tell readers to stop a GLP-1
- We do not recommend specific telehealth providers
- We do not accept money from drug manufacturers
- We do not accept money from telehealth clinics for editorial coverage
- We do not write content that exists to drive an affiliate commission
- We do not optimize for conversion on medical decisions

We optimize for *being right* and *being useful*. Trust is the asset. Trust is the moat. Trust is the only thing that's worth more in 5 years than it is today.
